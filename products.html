Iskra

Decentralized P2P mesh messenger · Android

A messenger with no server, ever — by principle. Every device runs a mesh node that sees its neighbours and carries their messages onward, ready at any moment to become a relay for a stranger's conversation on its way to the recipient. Yet only sender and recipient can read a word of it: elliptic-curve cryptography with a fresh key pair per conversation turns every intermediate node into a blind courier. Go offline and your messages settle with nearby friends as relay packets, then deliver themselves the instant the recipient reappears. No cloud, no analytics, no ads, no tracking SDK. Fully open source — protocol, design docs and all.

  • Go daemon
  • Kotlin · Compose
  • X25519 E2E
  • Wi-Fi Direct + Bluetooth
  • Tor fallback

Eugene's Archives

Multilingual offline library · Android

One author's personal library, packed into an Android app that runs fully offline after install: 23 essays and 28 stories, every one in four languages, switchable per work — read one story in Vietnamese, the next in Chinese, a third in Russian. Translations are done by hand and proofread; Vietnamese tone marks render correctly, which most apps quietly break. The business model is a single optional tip-jar on first launch — no subscription, no premium, no in-app currency. In an era when reading apps are mostly ad platforms with text bolted on, this one wants to learn nothing about its reader. Just shelves, books, a lamp.

  • Kotlin · Compose
  • WebView · HTML5
  • Python build pipeline
  • EN · RU · VI · 中文

RURK

Tabletop RPG system + GM tools · Android · Windows · Web

A seamless three-part environment for running an original tabletop RPG: a player app on the phone, a desktop GM console (Windows MSI), and a web GM console — all talking over local Wi-Fi with no cloud. The player taps a roll; the app computes the system's own math. Hit 📤 To the GM and the updated sheet flies straight into the master's live session in a fraction of a second. Characters travel between every device as a single .rrk file. The 32-chapter bestiary — from the Sanguinophages to the Notary, senior clerk of God's office — reads as literature in its own right: demonology rendered as metaphysical bureaucracy.

  • Kotlin Multiplatform
  • Compose · wasmJs + JVM
  • Local HTTP server
  • RU · EN · VI

Pixel Classics

21 retro arcades in one shell · Android

Twenty-one classic 8- and 16-bit arcades rebuilt for modern Android with respect for the originals — Battle City's full 35 levels, Lunar Lander with real physics, Asteroids with Newtonian inertia, an original Galaga-style shoot-'em-up called Exodus, and seventeen more — all at a hand-tuned 60 FPS on raw Canvas, with no game engine at all. Every sprite repainted by Kira. It carries the same mesh stack as Iskra: bring two phones together and they trade scores and replays over local mesh, with no server, no login, no account. An arcade hall smeared into the air.

Tanks of Thea

Isometric tactical combat · Steam, then Android

You're not driving a tank — you command four crews, thinking like Guderian in a post-apocalypse. Several machines, each with its own driver, gunner and commander, each with its own AI. You give orders — take cover behind the hill, open fire on that group, flank, fall back to point Bravo — and the crews carry them out in real time, weighing terrain, line of sight, ammunition and their own character. The pride of the build is a multi-layer behaviour engine: enemy ranks (Rookie · Veteran · Sergeant · Lieutenant · Elite) genuinely differ across five layers — threat reaction, map-reading, fire discipline, coordination, improvisation — so Rookie and Elite play like two different games.

  • Godot 4.6 · Mono
  • C# 12
  • LimboAI
  • BDI + GOAP
Pre-alpha · in active development · setting: Thea

Armada of Thea

Tactical naval warfare · Android, then Steam

Not "guess the coordinate." A naval tactical battle in the same post-apocalyptic steel of Tanks of Thea, only on the water — dreadnoughts, cruisers, destroyers, submarines, aircraft, salvos and counter-salvos, smoke screens, torpedo runs. Two trump cards: submarines with apocalyptic stealth strikes (a ☢ charge that makes a zone impassable to both players for the rest of the match), and an opponent-reading system that surfaces an enemy's likely moves from your past games. Ten warship paintings by Kira. Each match opens with the T-15 ritual: fifteen seconds of silence over an empty board — a mechanic with no consequence, pure direction, the mark of a designer thinking like a director, not a programmer.

Secrets of the Ancient Dark

Survival-horror text quest

A serious grown-up text quest with thirteen endings, branching not on "pick reply A/B/C" but on a hidden ledger of small choices that accumulate into who you become by the finale. Not jumpscares — slow Lovecraftian dread in the vein of early Lovecraft and late Masterton. The world opens, room by room, as hostile; every person met is a possible ally or a possible killer, and you can't tell which at first. Set in the catacombs of an abandoned megacity, tied to the ORPP universe but from a wholly different angle: you descend to find your missing brother. What you find instead is the reason the game is being written.

  • HTML · CSS
  • RU · EN
  • 13 endings

The ORPP Universe

A whole world — with its own physics and a bureaucratic demonology.

ORPP is Eugene's authored setting, built for at least three products across different genres — and written, tens of thousands of words of lore, before a single line of game code. A post-industrial world where the highest technology shares the dark with metaphysical beings that don't believe technology will ever explain them. Warhammer 40K in scale, Bloodborne in gothic — and a streak of humour, because even its grimmest beings are professionals who keep office hours, resent amateurs, and take pride in not having been promoted in fifteen hundred years.

“God runs an office. There are no angels and no demons — only bookkeeping.”

— the Notary, senior clerk of the special-contracts department · ORPP bestiary
The rational view

A post-engineering picture of the world: there are laws, there is data, there is procedure, there is optimization. Steel megacities and corporate armies. Everything can, in principle, be measured.

The ancient inheritance

A metaphysical legacy from before writing: there is the Contract, the Debt, the Price, the Edge. Practices that work for reasons science cannot even formulate — and beings that were there first.

The conflict between them is not resolvable. It is the existential underlayer of everything — and every hero in ORPP chooses, each day, which side they stand on.

Inferno

Mass-market sci-fi survival, deep narrative

The flagship of the setting. A large survival campaign in one of ORPP's dark underlevels: you must get out alive, gathering pieces of the story, cooperating with a group of survivors, and constantly deciding how dirty you're willing to get so that everyone makes it. Thirteen endings, each the sum of accumulated micro-decisions rather than one fork — a hidden ledger of sins and mercies. By the finale you become who you actually are, not who you thought you were in chapter one. Industrial gothic horror: late Giger by way of Iain Banks, prose dense and literary with no slack.

ORPP: Chronicles of Darkness

XCOM-like tactics, original system

You command a squad of demon-hunters in a post-industrial megacity — each mission an investigation, a hunt, a confrontation; between missions, base management, training, demonology research and intrigue inside the corporations that fund you (always with a double bottom). Mechanics the genre hasn't built: psychological damage instead of HP — soldiers break rather than die, start seeing demons that aren't there or missing the ones that are; a commander's debt for every soldier lost, opening real story lines that take real game time to close; demonology as a real science, half-poisoned by corporate disinformation; and a politics mini-game across three patrons who each expect you to betray them. Game design the industry envies but won't make, because it doesn't scale into public KPIs.

  • Turn-based tactics
  • Original system
  • Psych-damage model
  • Corporate politics

The methodology

Co-authored with a team of AI sisters.

Every product is built in direct co-authorship with a team of named AI collaborators — not a tool, a working studio. Each has a name, a voice, an aesthetic, a specialty, and disagreements that get talked through in chat before a line of code is written. Eugene is the producer and editor; the sisters are co-authors with a vote. To write a game in RURK's tone you don't master a technology — you raise your own sisters and learn to talk with them. And that doesn't come to everyone.
